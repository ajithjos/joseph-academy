# Household Arithmetic Fact Fluency Brief

This is a worked example of the [Curriculum slice brief template](../curriculum-slice-brief-template.md).

It is an example brief, not the canonical product architecture. For the object model and source-format guidance, read [Planning, authoring, and runtime](../../architecture/planning-authoring-and-runtime.md).

Use this as the first real curriculum slice for Cornerstone.

The goal is not to cover all of maths. The goal is to get two young learners into confident daily arithmetic with a sequence that is easy for a parent to assign, teach, and review.

## Identity

- Subject id: `maths`
- Subject title: Maths
- Area id: `arithmetic`
- Area title: Arithmetic

## Learner Context

- Target age range: 7 to 9
- Typical current level: can count and solve some written sums, but core facts are not yet automatic
- Why this area matters now: secure number facts make later written arithmetic much easier and reduce frustration in daily work
- Common learner mistakes or blockers:
  - counting in ones instead of recalling facts
  - weak number bonds to 10 and 20
  - confusion between addition and subtraction as inverse operations
  - knowing one multiplication fact in isolation but not the related division fact
  - freezing when questions are mixed instead of grouped by one table

## Desired Outcome

- What should the learner be able to do after this slice?
  - recall number bonds and simple addition and subtraction facts without heavy finger counting
  - answer early multiplication facts confidently in short mixed practice
  - move from paper practice to short computer-based drills without confusion
- What should a parent or coach notice in real life?
  - the learner answers familiar facts faster
  - the learner stalls less often during written arithmetic
  - the learner can explain simple relationships such as `7 + 3 = 10`, so `10 - 3 = 7`
- What counts as weak, partial, or secure performance?
  - weak: still counts most facts one by one, misses many facts in mixed practice, or cannot explain inverse pairs
  - partial: succeeds in grouped practice but slows down or breaks down in mixed review
  - secure: answers most facts accurately in mixed sets, with only occasional hesitation, and can use inverse relationships sensibly

## Candidate Stages

### Stage: Addition And Subtraction Facts To 10

- What it means in plain language: the learner knows small number relationships well enough that basic sums and take-away facts do not feel new every time
- Skills that belong inside it:
  - number bonds within 5
  - number bonds within 10
  - add within 10
  - subtract within 10

### Stage: Addition And Subtraction Facts To 20

- What it means in plain language: the learner can handle facts through 20, including missing-number questions, without depending on slow counting
- Skills that belong inside it:
  - number bonds within 20
  - add within 20
  - subtract within 20
  - missing-number addition and subtraction within 20

### Stage: Multiplication Facts Foundations

- What it means in plain language: the learner starts with the easiest, most useful multiplication patterns and sees them often enough to become automatic
- Skills that belong inside it:
  - multiply by 2
  - multiply by 5
  - multiply by 10
  - related division facts from 2, 5, and 10 tables

### Stage: Multiplication Facts Through 5

- What it means in plain language: the learner can handle mixed multiplication facts using factors up to 5 without needing a full recitation prompt
- Skills that belong inside it:
  - multiply by 3
  - multiply by 4
  - mixed multiplication facts through 5 x 5
  - related division facts from known tables

### Stage: Multiplication Facts Through 10

- What it means in plain language: the learner extends from early secure tables into the wider set needed for later arithmetic, but only after the earlier facts are steady
- Skills that belong inside it:
  - multiply by 6, 7, 8, and 9
  - mixed multiplication facts through 10 x 10
  - inverse division facts from known tables

## Candidate Skills

### Addition And Subtraction Facts To 10

- Skill title: Number bonds within 5
  - successful performance: gives complements within 5 quickly in mixed order
  - likely mistakes or misconceptions: recounts from 1 every time or treats each prompt as unrelated
  - out of scope: written column methods
- Skill title: Number bonds within 10
  - successful performance: gives pairs to 10 and simple missing-number facts quickly
  - likely mistakes or misconceptions: knows only a few anchor pairs such as `5 + 5`
  - out of scope: bridging through 10 with two-digit numbers
- Skill title: Add within 10
  - successful performance: answers mixed addition facts within 10 accurately and with little delay
  - likely mistakes or misconceptions: counts all objects instead of using known bonds
  - out of scope: two-digit addition
- Skill title: Subtract within 10
  - successful performance: answers mixed subtraction facts within 10 accurately and can explain the matching addition fact
  - likely mistakes or misconceptions: reverses the numbers or guesses from the addition fact without understanding
  - out of scope: regrouping or written subtraction algorithms

### Addition And Subtraction Facts To 20

- Skill title: Number bonds within 20
  - successful performance: solves complement and missing-number prompts within 20 with reasonable speed
  - likely mistakes or misconceptions: depends too heavily on counting on
  - out of scope: larger place-value decomposition
- Skill title: Add within 20
  - successful performance: answers mixed addition facts within 20 accurately, especially around 10 and 20 anchors
  - likely mistakes or misconceptions: fails on near-doubles or teen-number combinations
  - out of scope: formal written methods
- Skill title: Subtract within 20
  - successful performance: answers mixed subtraction facts within 20 and uses inverse reasoning when helpful
  - likely mistakes or misconceptions: counts backwards one by one or mixes up minuend and subtrahend
  - out of scope: negative numbers and formal regrouping

### Multiplication Facts

- Skill title: Multiply by 2, 5, and 10
  - successful performance: answers grouped and mixed facts from these tables quickly and accurately
  - likely mistakes or misconceptions: can chant the table but cannot answer when prompts are shuffled
  - out of scope: written multiplication
- Skill title: Multiply by 3 and 4
  - successful performance: answers short mixed sets correctly without reciting the full table aloud first
  - likely mistakes or misconceptions: confuses nearby facts such as `3 x 4` and `4 x 4`
  - out of scope: factors above 5 in mixed work
- Skill title: Mixed multiplication facts through 5 x 5
  - successful performance: answers at least 16 out of 20 mixed facts correctly in one short check
  - likely mistakes or misconceptions: knows grouped tables but loses accuracy in mixed review
  - out of scope: division notation if multiplication is not yet steady
- Skill title: Mixed multiplication facts through 10 x 10
  - successful performance: answers at least 18 out of 20 mixed facts correctly in one short check
  - likely mistakes or misconceptions: over-relies on skip counting or stalls on 6, 7, 8, and 9 facts
  - out of scope: 11 and 12 tables for the first household MVP

## Materials And Delivery

- likely material types:
  - `teaching_note` for the parent
  - `worksheet` for paper practice
  - short review sheets for mixed recall
  - one short check sheet per stage
  - later, one computer-based drill or quick check for randomised practice
- expected session length: 10 to 15 minutes
- expected cadence or number of sessions:
  - 4 to 5 sessions per week
  - 1 teaching or guided practice session
  - 2 to 3 short practice sessions
  - 1 mixed review or check session
- where review or recap should appear:
  - every playlist should include one mixed review session before moving on
  - weak facts should reappear in later sessions even after a stage looks mostly secure
- notes that a parent, teacher, or coach will need during delivery:
  - stop a session before frustration climbs too high
  - prefer speed after accuracy, not before it
  - keep oral recall, paper practice, and quick checks separate enough that the learner knows the purpose of each
  - use the computer for short random drills only after the underlying fact set has been taught clearly

## Constraints And Open Questions

- vocabulary or tone constraints:
  - use direct parent-facing language
  - prefer `addition facts` or `number bonds` over `addition table`
  - both `times tables` and `multiplication tables` are acceptable, but `times tables` is the more natural phrase for young learners
- family, classroom, or accessibility constraints:
  - this is household teaching, not a classroom program
  - sessions should work with paper first and computer second
  - no voice input or handwriting recognition is required for MVP
- anything that must not be generated:
  - long abstract explanations
  - oversized playlists that try to cover all arithmetic at once
  - tightly coupled gamification or reward mechanics
- unresolved questions that need answers before authoring continues:
  - should the seven-year-old and nine-year-old share one sequence with different entry points, or have separate playlists from day one?
  - which facts are already secure for each child?
  - do you want one shared parent-teaching note per stage or one per playlist?
