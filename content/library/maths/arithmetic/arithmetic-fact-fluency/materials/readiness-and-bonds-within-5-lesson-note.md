# Readiness And Bonds Within 5 Lesson Note

This lesson teaches your eyes and your brain to work together.

Look fast. Say the whole. Then say the parts.

## Why this matters

If you always count from `1`, every new question feels heavy.

If you can see a small group and know its parts, you can answer tiny addition and subtraction facts much faster.

## Small groups you should know

- `1` is one thing.
- `2` can be `1 and 1`.
- `3` can be `2 and 1`.
- `4` can be `2 and 2` or `3 and 1`.
- `5` can be `4 and 1` or `3 and 2`.

Say the whole first, then the parts.

Example:

- `I see 4.`
- `4 is 3 and 1.`

## Pairs that make 5

These are the number bonds to `5`.

- `0 and 5 make 5`
- `1 and 4 make 5`
- `2 and 3 make 5`
- `3 and 2 make 5`
- `4 and 1 make 5`
- `5 and 0 make 5`

## One tiny story, several facts

If `2 and 3 make 5`, then you also know:

- `2 + 3 = 5`
- `3 + 2 = 5`
- `5 - 2 = 3`
- `5 - 3 = 2`

That is a fact family. The numbers stay the same. Only the question changes.

## Worked examples

### Example 1: `1 + 4`

- `1 and 4 make 5`
- so `1 + 4 = 5`

### Example 2: `5 - 2`

- `2 and 3 make 5`
- so `5 - 2 = 3`

### Example 3: `4 - 1`

- `1 and 3 make 4`
- so `4 - 1 = 3`

## Good learner self-talk

- `I can see the whole first.`
- `I know the parts inside 5.`
- `Subtraction asks for the missing part.`

## Quick spoken practice

1. `2 and __ make 5`
2. `1 + 3 =`
3. `5 - 4 =`
4. `3 and __ make 5`
5. `2 + 2 =`
6. `4 - 3 =`

Come back to this note until the pairs to `5` feel obvious and small groups stop needing a recount.
